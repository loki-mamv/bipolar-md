Bipolar I Disorder

The Classic Form

Defining feature: At least one manic episode lasting 7+ days (or any duration if hospitalization is required).

Bipolar I is characterized by full manic episodesâ€”periods of extremely elevated mood, energy, and activity that represent a clear change from normal behavior. These episodes can include:

  • Feeling "high" or euphoric for extended periods
  • Severe irritability
  • Sleeping very little (3 hours) yet feeling energized
  • Racing thoughts and rapid speech
  • Grandiose beliefs (e.g., special powers or importance)
  • Impulsive, risky behavior (spending sprees, sexual indiscretion, poor business decisions)
  • In severe cases, psychotic features (delusions, hallucinations)

Most people with Bipolar I also experience depressive episodes, but depression isn't required for diagnosis. The manic episode alone is sufficient.

Bipolar II Disorder

The Underdiagnosed Form

Defining feature: Hypomanic episodes (less severe than mania) plus major depressive episodes. Never a full manic episode.

Bipolar II is often misdiagnosed as major depression because people usually seek help during depressive episodes, not when they're hypomanic (which often feels good). Key characteristics:

  • Hypomania: Elevated mood and energy, but still functioning. Others notice you're "not yourself," but it doesn't severely impair work or relationships.
  • More time depressed: The depression in Bipolar II can be as severe as in Bipolar I, and people often spend significantly more time depressed than hypomanic.
  • Higher suicide risk: Despite being "milder," Bipolar II has a higher rate of suicide attempts, possibly because of the predominance of depression.

Treating Bipolar II with antidepressants alone (without a mood stabilizer) can trigger hypomania or rapid cycling. Proper diagnosis ensures proper treatment.

Cyclothymic Disorder (Cyclothymia)

The Chronic Mild Form

Defining feature: Chronic fluctuating mood with numerous periods of hypomanic and depressive symptoms that don't meet criteria for full episodes. Must persist for at least 2 years in adults.

Cyclothymia involves less severe symptoms but more constant mood instability:

  • Never symptom-free for more than 2 months
  • Mood swings that others might dismiss as "just your personality"
  • Can progress to Bipolar I or II in 15-50% of cases
  • Often affects relationships and career due to unpredictability

Other Specified Bipolar Disorder

This category includes bipolar presentations that don't fit neatly into the three main types. Examples:

  • Hypomania lasting less than 4 days
  • Hypomanic episodes without a history of major depression
  • Recurrent hypomanic episodes without depressive episodes
  • Symptoms that cause distress but don't meet full criteria

How Are Types Diagnosed?

Diagnosis is based on clinical evaluation, including:

  1. Detailed history: Past mood episodes, duration, severity, and impact on functioning
  2. Family history: Bipolar disorder runs in families
  3. Rule out other causes: Thyroid conditions, substance use, and other psychiatric conditions can mimic bipolar
  4. Mood charting: Tracking daily mood over time can reveal patterns

There's no blood test or brain scan that diagnoses bipolar disorder. It requires a comprehensive evaluation by a qualified mental health professional.

Does Type Matter for Treatment?

Yes, type influences treatment approach:

  • Bipolar I: Mood stabilizers (lithium) and/or atypical antipsychotics are typically first-line. Antidepressants used cautiously.
  • Bipolar II: Mood stabilizers preferred. Some evidence for lamotrigine for depression. Antidepressants carefully considered with mood stabilizer coverage.
  • Cyclothymia: May be managed with lower doses of mood stabilizers. Psychotherapy plays a larger role.

Regardless of type, all forms benefit from psychotherapy, lifestyle management (sleep, stress, routine), and ongoing monitoring.
