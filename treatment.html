Bipolar Disorder Treatment

Updated Feb 2026 · 15 min read

Bipolar disorder is a lifelong condition, but it's highly treatable. The right combination of medication, therapy, and lifestyle management can lead to long periods of stability.

Medication: The Foundation

Most people with bipolar disorder need medication to achieve and maintain stability. The goal is to prevent both manic/hypomanic and depressive episodes while minimizing side effects.

Mood Stabilizers

Lithium

Brand names: Lithobid, Eskalith

The gold standard for bipolar treatment since the 1970s. Effective for both mania and depression, and the only medication proven to reduce suicide risk in bipolar disorder.

Monitoring required: Regular blood tests for lithium levels, kidney function, and thyroid function.

Common side effects: Thirst, frequent urination, tremor, weight gain, cognitive dulling

Valproate/Divalproex

Brand names: Depakote, Depakene

Effective for acute mania and maintenance. Often better tolerated than lithium for some patients. Works faster than lithium for acute episodes.

Common side effects: Weight gain, sedation, tremor, hair loss, GI upset. Not safe during pregnancy.

Lamotrigine

Brand names: Lamictal

Particularly effective for bipolar depression and maintenance. Less effective for acute mania. Often well-tolerated with minimal weight gain.

Must titrate slowly to reduce risk of serious skin rash (Stevens-Johnson syndrome).

Common side effects: Headache, dizziness, rash (report immediately)

Atypical Antipsychotics

These medications treat acute episodes and provide maintenance. They work faster than traditional mood stabilizers for acute mania.

Quetiapine (Seroquel)

FDA-approved for bipolar mania, depression, and maintenance. One of the most versatile bipolar medications.

Common side effects: Sedation, weight gain, metabolic effects

Aripiprazole (Abilify)

FDA-approved for mania and maintenance. Generally less sedating and less weight gain than other antipsychotics.

Common side effects: Restlessness (akathisia), insomnia

Other Options

Olanzapine (Zyprexa), risperidone (Risperdal), lurasidone (Latuda), and cariprazine (Vraylar) also have FDA approval for various phases of bipolar disorder. Each has different side effect profiles.

⚠️ Antidepressants: Proceed With Caution

Antidepressants can trigger mania or rapid cycling in bipolar disorder. If used at all, they should always be paired with a mood stabilizer or antipsychotic. Many bipolar specialists prefer to avoid them entirely.

Psychotherapy

Medication manages symptoms, but therapy teaches skills, improves relationships, and helps you understand your condition. Research supports several approaches:

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT)

Helps identify and change negative thought patterns and behaviors. Teaches coping skills for managing symptoms and preventing relapse. Can help address sleep problems, substance use, and other co-occurring issues.

Interpersonal and Social Rhythm Therapy (IPSRT)

Specifically developed for bipolar disorder. Focuses on stabilizing daily routines (sleep, meals, activity) and improving relationships. Helps identify triggers and early warning signs.

Family-Focused Therapy

Involves family members in treatment. Improves communication, reduces conflict, and teaches family members to recognize warning signs and support recovery. Particularly effective for younger patients.

Psychoeducation

Structured education about bipolar disorder—what it is, how treatment works, early warning signs, and self-management strategies. Even simple psychoeducation reduces relapse rates.

Lifestyle Management

These aren't optional extras—lifestyle factors are critical to stability:

Sleep

Sleep disruption is both a trigger and early warning sign. Prioritize:

  • Regular sleep and wake times (even on weekends)
  • 7-9 hours per night
  • Treating sleep disorders if present
  • Alerting your doctor if sleep patterns change significantly

Routine

Predictable daily schedules stabilize circadian rhythms and reduce episode risk. Structure meals, exercise, social activities, and work around consistent times.

Substance Avoidance

Alcohol and drugs worsen bipolar disorder and interfere with medication. Caffeine can disrupt sleep. Cannabis may trigger or worsen episodes in some people.

Stress Management

Major life stressors can trigger episodes. While you can't eliminate stress, you can build resilience through therapy, social support, and stress-reduction techniques.

Other Treatments

Electroconvulsive Therapy (ECT)

Highly effective for severe or treatment-resistant episodes, especially severe depression or catatonia. Modern ECT is safe and has fewer side effects than portrayed in media. Memory side effects are usually temporary.

Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation (TMS)

FDA-approved for depression. Some evidence for bipolar depression, but less studied than ECT. Typically used when medications aren't working well enough.

Finding the Right Treatment

It often takes time and patience to find the right medication combination. Tips for working with your treatment team:

  • Be honest about symptoms, side effects, and whether you're taking medications as prescribed
  • Keep a mood chart to track patterns and medication effects
  • Don't stop medications without discussing with your doctor—even if you feel fine
  • Report side effects early—often they can be managed with dose adjustments or medication changes
  • Give medications time—full effects may take weeks to months
