Mood Tracking for Bipolar Disorder

Last reviewed: February 2026 ¬∑ 7 min read

Mood tracking is one of the most powerful tools for managing bipolar disorder. By monitoring patterns over time, you can identify triggers, catch early warning signs, and work with your care team to prevent episodes.

Why Track Your Mood?

Bipolar mood episodes often have warning signs days or weeks before they fully develop. Regular tracking helps you:

  • Spot patterns ‚Äî Link mood shifts to sleep, stress, seasons, or medication changes
  • Catch episodes early ‚Äî Identify prodromal symptoms before a full episode
  • Communicate with providers ‚Äî Give your psychiatrist objective data, not just memory
  • Evaluate treatment ‚Äî See if medication changes are actually helping
  • Build self-awareness ‚Äî Understand your unique bipolar pattern

What to Track

The most useful mood charts capture multiple dimensions, not just "good day" or "bad day."

üìä Mood Level

Rate from -5 (severely depressed) through 0 (stable) to +5 (severely manic). Record at the same time daily.

üò¥ Sleep

Hours slept and quality. Reduced need for sleep is an early mania sign. Oversleeping often precedes depression.

‚ö° Energy

Separate from mood ‚Äî you can have high energy with neutral mood (hypomania warning) or low energy with okay mood.

üò∞ Anxiety/Irritability

Common in mixed episodes and can signal either mania or depression brewing.

üíä Medications

Missed doses, timing changes, new medications. Critical for correlation.

üìù Events/Triggers

Major stressors, travel, schedule disruptions, arguments, positive events.

The Mood Scale

A standardized scale makes your data more useful:

-5 Severe Depression 0 Stable +5 Severe Mania
  • -5 to -3: Severe depression ‚Äî difficulty functioning, may need urgent care
  • -2 to -1: Mild depression ‚Äî low mood, reduced motivation, still functioning
  • 0: Stable/euthymic ‚Äî baseline mood
  • +1 to +2: Hypomania ‚Äî elevated mood, more energy, still in control
  • +3 to +5: Mania ‚Äî impaired judgment, may need intervention

Tips for Consistent Tracking

  • Same time daily ‚Äî Set a phone reminder for the same time each day
  • Keep it quick ‚Äî Under 2 minutes or you'll stop doing it
  • Track even when stable ‚Äî Baseline data is just as valuable
  • Don't overthink ratings ‚Äî First instinct is usually right
  • Review weekly ‚Äî Look for patterns every Sunday

üí° Share With Your Provider

Bring 2-4 weeks of mood data to every psychiatry appointment. It's far more useful than trying to remember how you felt. Many apps can email reports directly.

Early Warning Signs to Watch

Tracking helps you identify your personal early warning signs. Common patterns:

Mania/Hypomania Coming

  • Sleep decreasing without feeling tired
  • Racing thoughts, rapid speech
  • Increased productivity/goal-directed activity
  • More social than usual
  • Increased spending or risk-taking urges

Depression Coming

  • Sleeping more but still tired
  • Withdrawing from friends/activities
  • Things that usually bring joy don't
  • Difficulty concentrating
  • Negative thought patterns increasing

Using Data to Prevent Episodes

Once you identify patterns, you can intervene early:

  1. Create an action plan ‚Äî "If sleep drops below 5 hours for 2 nights, call psychiatrist"
  2. Know your triggers ‚Äî Avoid or prepare for known destabilizers
  3. Involve support people ‚Äî Share warning signs with trusted family/friends
  4. Adjust proactively ‚Äî Your doctor may have PRN medications for early intervention
